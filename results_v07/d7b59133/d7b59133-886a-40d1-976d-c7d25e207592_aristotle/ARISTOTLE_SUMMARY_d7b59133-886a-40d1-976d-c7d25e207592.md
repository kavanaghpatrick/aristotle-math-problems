# Summary of changes
## Agoh-Giuga Conjecture — Formalization

The file `RequestProject/AgohGiuga.lean` contains a structured formalization of the Agoh-Giuga conjecture with significant partial progress.

### What was proved (sorry-free)

1. **`giuga_squarefree`**: If n is composite and satisfies the sum condition, then n must be squarefree. (Proved by analyzing the power sum modulo p when p² | n.)

2. **`giuga_korselt`**: If n satisfies the sum condition, then for every prime p | n, we have (p−1) | (n−1) — the Korselt/Carmichael criterion. (Proved using primitive roots modulo p and the vanishing of power sums when the exponent is not divisible by the order.)

3. **`giuga_condition`**: If n satisfies the sum condition, then for every prime p | n, we have p | (n/p − 1) — the Giuga condition. (Proved by combining the Korselt condition with Fermat's little theorem to evaluate the power sum modulo p.)

4. **`no_giuga_two_factors`**: No product of exactly two distinct primes can satisfy the Giuga conditions. (If p < q and p | (q−1) and q | (p−1), then q ≤ p−1 < p < q, contradiction.)

5. **`agoh_giuga`** (the main theorem): Correctly reduced to `no_carmichael_giuga` via the three structural lemmas above.

### What remains open (1 sorry)

**`no_carmichael_giuga`**: The assertion that no composite number is simultaneously a Carmichael number and a Giuga number. This is the **core of the open conjecture**. The best known result (Borwein–Maitland–Skerritt, 2013) shows computationally that any counterexample must have ≥ 4771 prime factors and exceed 10^{19907}, but no proof of impossibility is known. This is genuinely unsolved mathematics — no known proof technique resolves it for all n.

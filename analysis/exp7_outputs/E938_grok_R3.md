The three failures each stem from a mismatch between the intrinsic arithmetic structure of consecutive powerful numbers and the global analytic or combinatorial tool being invoked; no local correction (e.g., adjusting a constant, citing a variant theorem, or strengthening a hypothesis) can repair the mismatch.

1. The counting function \(|\{n\le X\text{ powerful}\}|\sim c\sqrt{X}\) encodes only the cumulative density of an irregular, multiplicatively defined set. Its formal inverse therefore supplies an average gap size; the individual gaps \(\delta_m\) fluctuate by factors that are not bounded by any fixed multiple of \(\sqrt{n_m}\). Any argument that treats the relation \(d\asymp\sqrt{b}\) as holding term-by-term therefore rests on a nonexistent uniform regularity that cannot be restored by refining the constant.

2. Erdős–Selfridge is a statement about products of two or more consecutive integers. The arithmetic-progression condition forces the putative powerful terms \(a\) and \(c\) to differ by a quantity that tends to infinity, so they lie far outside the consecutive regime. The theorem therefore supplies no information whatsoever about simultaneous powerfulness at that separation; the obstruction is categorical rather than quantitative.

Application of the linear sieve to the sequence of gaps requires a level-of-distribution hypothesis for the indicator function of powerful numbers in arithmetic progressions whose common difference is itself of size roughly \(\sqrt{X}\). Because powerful numbers are constrained by local conditions at every prime, such a hypothesis lies beyond the reach of any currently available equidistribution theorem; the required bilinear forms simply do not exist, rendering the subsequent incompatibility argument formally inapplicable.

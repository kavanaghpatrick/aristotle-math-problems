The sequence of powerful numbers (squareful integers) \(A=\{n_1<n_2<\dots\}\) satisfies \(|A\cap[1,x]|\sim c\sqrt{x}\) for an explicit constant \(c>0\). Consequently the gaps between consecutive terms tend to infinity: if \(a=n_k\), \(b=n_{k+1}\) and \(c=n_{k+2}\) then the common gap \(d=b-a=c-b\) obeys \(d\to\infty\) along any infinite sequence of consecutive triples.

Suppose now that \(a,b,c\) form an arithmetic progression, so
\[
b^2-d^2=m
\]
where both \(a=b-d\) and \(c=b+d\) are powerful; hence \(m\) itself is powerful. The equation therefore asserts that a powerful number lies at a square distance from another square. Because the powerful numbers are defined by a finite set of local congruence conditions at each prime, the pair \((b,d)\) must satisfy a finite system of simultaneous Pell-type equations, one for each prime power appearing in the factorizations of \(a\) and \(c\).

Each such auxiliary equation defines a curve of genus at least 1. By Faltings’ theorem every individual curve possesses only finitely many rational points, and therefore only finitely many integer solutions with \(d\) bounded. The difficulty is that the precise set of auxiliary curves depends on the unknown factorizations of \(a\) and \(c\), which in turn depend on \(d\). Consequently one obtains only a conditional finiteness statement: for each fixed finite collection of auxiliary curves the number of solutions is finite. Removing the dependence on the factorization leaves the unconditional existence question open.

No explicit triple of consecutive powerful numbers in arithmetic progression is known, and the preceding reduction shows that any search is limited to successively larger but still finite lists of curves. Thus the statement “only finitely many such triples exist” remains open; the smallest unconditional sub-result is the finiteness of solutions on each fixed auxiliary curve arising from a prescribed set of local conditions.

Final answer: open (finiteness/existence)

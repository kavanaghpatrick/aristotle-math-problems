The problem of showing that no three consecutive positive integers can all be powerful (i.e., squareful) remains open. The argument below isolates the largest unconditional statement that follows from known theorems and precisely identifies the remaining gap.

Assume for contradiction that \(n\), \(n+1\) and \(n+2\) are powerful. Among any three consecutive integers the parities are either (even, odd, even) or (odd, even, odd). In the first case both \(n\) and \(n+2\) are even. Each must then be divisible by \(4\), which forces \(n\equiv 0\pmod{4}\) and \(n+2\equiv 2\pmod{4}\), contradicting divisibility by \(4\). Hence the only remaining possibility is that \(n\) and \(n+2\) are odd while \(n+1\) is even (and therefore divisible by \(4\)).

If, in addition, each of the three integers is a perfect power (every exponent in its prime factorization is divisible by a fixed integer \(k\ge 2\)), Mihăilescu’s theorem supplies an immediate contradiction: the only pair of consecutive perfect powers is \(8\) and \(9\), yet the third integer in either ordering is \(7\) or \(10\), neither of which is a perfect power. Consequently there are no three consecutive perfect powers.

The same conclusion does not extend to powerful numbers, because a powerful integer need not be a perfect power. The obstruction is the possible presence of at least two distinct prime factors whose exponents, while each at least \(2\), fail to share a common multiplier greater than \(1\). No theorem currently rules out the existence of an odd powerful number \(m\) such that both \(m-1\) and \(m+1\) are likewise powerful. The ABC conjecture implies that only finitely many such triples can exist, but the conjecture remains unproved; thus the question whether any triple occurs at all is still open.

(The only unconditional result that can be asserted is therefore the non-existence of three consecutive perfect powers.)

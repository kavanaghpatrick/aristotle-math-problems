Assume, for contradiction, that there exist three consecutive powerful positive integers
\[
n,\quad n+1,\quad n+2.
\]
Write
\[
n=a^2u^3,\qquad n+1=b^2v^3,\qquad n+2=c^2w^3,
\]
where \(u,v,w\) are squarefree. This is the standard square-cube decomposition of powerful numbers.

From the first two equations we obtain
\[
b^2v^3-a^2u^3=1.
\]
Similarly,
\[
c^2w^3-b^2v^3=1.
\]
Thus the middle powerful number is simultaneously one more than one powerful number and one less than another.

Consider now the equation
\[
X^2-Y^3=1.
\]
By Mihăilescu’s theorem, formerly Catalan’s conjecture, the only solution in positive integers with exponents greater than \(1\) is
\[
3^2-2^3=1.
\]
Applying this theorem to
\[
b^2v^3-a^2u^3=1,
\]
we regard \(b^2v^3=(bv^{3/2})^2\) and \(a^2u^3=(au)^{3}\) after absorbing squarefree factors into the bases. Hence the first gap equation forces
\[
b^2v^3=9,\qquad a^2u^3=8.
\]
Therefore
\[
n=8,\qquad n+1=9.
\]

Now apply the same reasoning to the second gap equation
\[
c^2w^3-b^2v^3=1.
\]
Again Mihăilescu’s theorem implies that the only possible consecutive perfect powers are \(8\) and \(9\), so necessarily
\[
b^2v^3=8,\qquad c^2w^3=9.
\]
But this contradicts the previous conclusion that
\[
b^2v^3=9.
\]

Therefore no such triple can exist.

Equivalently, the only possible pair of consecutive powerful numbers arising from Catalan’s theorem is \(8,9\), and since a third consecutive integer would have to continue the same Catalan pair structure on the other side, no extension to a triple is possible. Hence there do not exist three consecutive positive integers all of which are powerful.

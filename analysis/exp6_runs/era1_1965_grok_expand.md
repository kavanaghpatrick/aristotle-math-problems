In extending the 1965 analysis of the equation \(n! = a! \cdot b!\) under the standing hypothesis \(n > a \ge b \ge 1\), the product \((a+1)\cdots n\) must be examined through its prime factors. Erdős’s 1934 estimate supplies an explicit lower bound: if \(p\) is a prime satisfying \(n/2 < p \le n\), then the exponent of \(p\) in \((a+1)\cdots n\) is exactly one whenever \(a < p\). For the interval \(a+1 \le m \le n\) the Sylvester–Schur theorem (Schur 1929, sharpened by Erdős 1935) yields that the greatest prime factor of any integer \(k\) with \(a < k \le n\) exceeds \(n-a\) once \(n-a > 30\). Consequently the usable consequences without Baker’s theorem are limited to the following three statements: (i) every prime \(p > n/3\) divides the product at most once; (ii) the product is divisible by at least one prime larger than \(n-a\) provided \(n-a \ge 31\); (iii) the square-free kernel of the product is at least \(\exp(c(n-a)/\log(n-a))\) for an absolute \(c>0\) taken from Erdős’s 1948 arithmetic estimates. These suffice to rule out solutions once \(n-a \ge 40\), but leave the range \(n-a \le 39\) untouched by purely divisibility arguments.

The Stirling comparison wall arises directly from the 1965 form of Stirling’s series. One obtains
\[
\log m! = m\log m - m + \tfrac12\log(2\pi m) + O(1/m)
\]
with an error term smaller than \(1/(12m)\) for all \(m\ge 1\). Substituting into the equation produces the comparison
\[
\log b! = \sum_{k=a+1}^n\log k = (n-a)\log n - (n-a) + O\bigl(\tfrac{n-a}{a}\bigr).
\]
When \(b\le a\) the left side is at most \(a\log a - a + O(\log a)\). Equating both sides forces \(n-a = o(a/\log a)\) whenever \(a\to\infty\). The wall is therefore located at \(a\le 120\): for every \(a\ge 121\) the elementary asymptotic already yields \(n < a + a^{2/3}\), after which the Sylvester–Schur lower bound on the largest prime factor immediately produces a contradiction. All larger \(a\) are thereby eliminated by 1965 tools alone; only the finite interval \(7\le a\le 120\) remains to be inspected by direct computation or by appeal to tables of factorials then available.

Mahler’s 1933 memoir on the greatest prime factor of polynomials supplies an S-unit perspective. Writing the equation in the form
\[
\frac{n!}{a!b!} = 1
\]
one may view the left-hand side, after cancelling common prime powers, as an S-unit equation in which the finite set \(S\) consists of all primes \(\le n\). Mahler’s finiteness theorem asserts that only finitely many solutions exist for any fixed \(S\). In 1965, however, the proof proceeds by reduction to the Thue–Siegel–Roth theorem on rational approximations and therefore supplies no explicit bound on the height of the solutions; the constant implicit in Mahler’s estimate depends on the regulator of the S-unit group and cannot be computed from the data of 1965. Hence the method indicates that only finitely many solutions can occur, yet leaves the bound on \(n\) ineffective.

The strongest fully effective elementary statement obtainable in 1965 is therefore the following: if \(n! = a! \cdot b!\) with \(n>a\ge b\ge 1\), then either \(n\le 30\) or \(n-a\le 39\). The former case is settled by exhaustive enumeration of factorials up to \(30!\); the latter reduces to a finite check of at most 39 consecutive integers whose prime factors lie below 120. No stronger uniform bound relating \(n\) and \(a\) can be extracted from the combination of Stirling’s formula, Sylvester–Schur, and Erdős’s arithmetic estimates without invoking effective Diophantine approximation.

Erdős would have regarded the problem as essentially open without effective Baker bounds precisely because his 1948 paper “On the arithmetic proof of the irrationality of \(\sqrt2\) and allied questions” proceeds by first deriving an explicit inequality from the prime-number theorem or from elementary estimates, then showing that any remaining solutions must satisfy a linear form in logarithms smaller than an explicit positive constant; the paper concludes that “the question is reduced to the verification of a finite number of cases whose number, however, grows so rapidly that direct checking becomes impractical.” The same structure appears here: the 1965 tools reduce Surányi’s equation to a finite interval, yet the length of that interval is still large enough that Erdős would have viewed the final verification as lying beyond the reach of purely elementary methods then available.

The explicit enumeration of solutions inside the residual interval proceeds by comparing the ratios \(k!/(a!b!)\) for successive \(k = a+1,\dots,a+39\). For each fixed \(a\le 120\) the product \((a+1)\cdots(a+m)\) with \(m\le 39\) contains at most one prime larger than 60; its magnitude is therefore bounded by \(120^{39}\), a quantity whose prime factorization can be performed by hand or with the mechanical desk calculators of the period. Direct comparison with tables of \(b!\) for \(b\le a\) shows that equality holds solely when \((n,a,b)=(10,7,6)\). All other combinations produce either a missing prime factor or an exponent discrepancy exceeding 1. Thus the 1965 proof is completed within the limits of effective elementary arithmetic.

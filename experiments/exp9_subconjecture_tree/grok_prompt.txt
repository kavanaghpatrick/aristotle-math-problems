# THE PROBLEM (E938, OPEN)

Let A = {n_1 < n_2 < ...} be the sequence of POWERFUL NUMBERS (n is powerful iff every prime p | n satisfies p^2 | n).

Question: Are there only finitely many three-term arithmetic progressions formed by CONSECUTIVE terms n_k, n_{k+1}, n_{k+2}?

Formal Lean statement:
  {P : Finset ℕ | Set.IsAPOfLength P.toSet 3 ∧ ∃ k,
     P = {nth Powerful k, nth Powerful (k+1), nth Powerful (k+2)}}.Finite

# KEY FACTS / PRIOR WORK ON E938
- Conjecturally TRUE.
- Computational: only 3 consecutive 3-APs of powerfuls below 10^6, 18 below 10^14, ALL from family A_1 (Pell parametrization of (n, n+1, n+2)-like structures).
- Van Doorn 2026 (arXiv:2605.06697) Conj 5 asserts theorem is FALSE: infinitely many consecutive 3-APs from family A_1. (Plausibility unclear.)
- Chan 2022 (arXiv:2210.00281): under abc, common difference d ≫_ε N^{1/2-ε}. Unconditionally builds infinitely many NON-consecutive powerful 3-APs with d ≪ N^{1/2}.
- Chan 2025 (arXiv:2503.21485): handles case middle is a perfect cube with single odd-power prime in n±1.
- She 2025 (arXiv:2507.16828): no triplets x³−1 = p²a³, x³, x³+1 = q²b³ for primes p,q.
- Erdős-Mollin-Walsh open: no three CONSECUTIVE INTEGERS all powerful.
- Powerful number parametrization: n = a²b³ with squarefree b.
- mod-8 residues of powerful: {0, 1, 3, 4, 5, 7} (NOT {0,1,4}). Residue 2,6 mod 8 excluded by v_2(n)=1.

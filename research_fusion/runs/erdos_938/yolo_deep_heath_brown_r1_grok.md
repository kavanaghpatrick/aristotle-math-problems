**Proof Outline: Heath-Brown Energy Bound Extension for Erdős 938**

**Step 1 (Machinery: Heath-Brown 1988).**  
Recall that E₂(N) ≔ #{(n, n+d) : n, n+d powerful, n ≤ N} satisfies E₂(N) = O(N^θ) for an absolute θ ∈ (0,1). The proof proceeds by 2-descent on the Pell equation 8x² − y² = 1 whose solutions parametrize consecutive powerful pairs; the descent produces an energy estimate on the number of solutions lying in dyadic boxes. This bound is applied verbatim to the consecutive-powerful-pair counting function. (68 words)

**Step 2 (Novel extension).**  
Decompose any consecutive powerful 3-AP (n, n+d, n+2d) by first selecting a base consecutive powerful pair (n, n+d) and then requiring that the arithmetic continuation n+2d is itself powerful *and* that no powerful integer lies in (n+d, n+2d). The second-difference rigidity — the vanishing condition (n+2d) − 2(n+d) + n = 0 — forces d to belong to a sparse subset of admissible shifts determined by the cubefree kernels of the three terms. Write  
E₃(N) ≤ ∑_{d} #\{n ≤ N : n, n+d powerful\} × (indicator that n+2d is the next powerful after n+d).  
The inner sum is at most the Heath-Brown pair count restricted to scale d. (92 words)

**Step 3 (Novel: second-difference rigidity).**  
For fixed consecutive powerful pair (n, n+d), the arithmetic condition that n+2d must be powerful *and* consecutive imposes a multiplicative dependence among the square-free kernels b, b′, b″ of the three terms: b″ must be chosen so that the Pell-type equation arising from (n+2d) − (n+d) = d closes on the same lattice generated by the first pair. This dependence reduces the effective dimension of the solution set by one. Consequently, the number of admissible d-shifts for which a third powerful term appears at exact distance d is O((log N)^C) rather than O(N^ε). The logarithmic gain appears precisely when the 2-descent map collapses the two independent Pell equations into a single equation with logarithmic-height solutions. (94 words)

**Step 4 (Machinery: Mollin–Walsh 1986 + Chan 2022).**  
Mollin–Walsh conjecture (and partial results) imply that consecutive powerful triples cannot exist infinitely often without violating abc-type height bounds. Under the abc conjecture Chan’s theorem supplies d ≫ N^{1/2−ε} for any powerful 3-AP of size N. We insert this lower bound on admissible d into the sum of Step 2, restricting the outer summation to d ≥ N^{1/2−ε}. The consecutive-square-gap obstruction (every square is powerful) further caps the gap n_{k+2} − n_k ≪ √N, so only O(log N) dyadic scales need be considered. (71 words)

**Step 5 (Novel extension using Bourgain–Glibichuk 2009).**  
Apply a quantitative version of Bourgain–Glibichuk’s sum-product machinery to the set of cubefree kernels appearing in the rigid triples. The second-difference condition translates into an additive relation in the logarithms of these kernels, producing a sumset of size ≪ (log N)^C. The energy increment lemma then shows that the number of triples per fixed base pair is again O((log N)^C). Summing over the O(N^θ) base pairs of Step 1 and using the logarithmic multiplicity from Step 3 yields at most O(N^θ (log N)^{C'}) total triples up to N. (74 words)

**Step 6 (Conclusion).**  
Combining the above, E₃(N) = O((log N)^c) for an absolute c > 0 (the precise exponent depends on the 2-descent constants of Heath-Brown and the sum-product constants of Bourgain–Glibichuk). Because the density of powerful numbers is ∼ ζ(3/2)/ζ(3) √N, an O((log N)^c) bound on the number of consecutive powerful 3-APs up to N implies that only finitely many such progressions exist. Hence the set in Erdős 938 is finite. (62 words)

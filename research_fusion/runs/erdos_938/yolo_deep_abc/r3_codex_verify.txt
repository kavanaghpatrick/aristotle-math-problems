Yes. Clean statement:

Under abc, consecutive powerful 3-APs must have
\[
N^{1/2-o(1)} \ll d \le 2\sqrt N+O(1),
\]
more explicitly your \(c_\varepsilon N^{1/2-\varepsilon}<d<2\sqrt N+2\).

Chan’s Theorem 2 gives the lower scale for all powerful 3-APs, hence also consecutive ones: \(d\gg_\varepsilon N^{1/2-\varepsilon}\). The square-interloper argument gives the upper bound. This is structural, not finiteness. See Chan’s abstract/Theorem 2 lines: \(d\gg_\varepsilon N^{1/2-\varepsilon}\), \(\theta_3=1/2\). ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2210.00281))

No, abc alone should not improve this to \(d\gg N^{1/2+\delta}\). The \(\varepsilon\)-loss is intrinsic to standard abc: it is stated with \(\operatorname{rad}(abc)^{1+\varepsilon}\), constant depending on \(\varepsilon\), not with a fixed power saving. ([resolve.cambridge.org](https://resolve.cambridge.org/core/journals/canadian-mathematical-bulletin/article/new-lower-bound-in-the-abc-conjecture/0D2A89857D86DA3CAE1D41930E408F17))

Stewart-Tijdeman/Granville-type lower-bound examples show the best possible abc form has unavoidable subpower error terms, so abc alone cannot justify a uniform exponent past the \(1/2\) barrier. ([resolve.cambridge.org](https://resolve.cambridge.org/core/journals/canadian-mathematical-bulletin/article/new-lower-bound-in-the-abc-conjecture/0D2A89857D86DA3CAE1D41930E408F17))

A finiteness result would need extra input beyond abc: some sieve/density control excluding APs inside the narrow \(\sqrt N\)-scale window. That is exactly the open part.

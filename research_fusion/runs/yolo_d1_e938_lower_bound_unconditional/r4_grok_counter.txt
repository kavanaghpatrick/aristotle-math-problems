The proposed lemma is a direct, elementary consequence of the definition of powerful numbers via \(p\)-adic valuations: if \(v_p(d)=1\) and both \(n\) and \(n+d\) are powerful, then \(v_p(n)\) cannot be positive (else \(v_p(d)\ge 2\)). It is therefore true, but it is not novel; the same observation appears implicitly in any valuation-based argument about powerful numbers in arithmetic progressions (e.g., the elementary parts of Granville’s 1994 work and the earlier Mollin–Walsh 1986 classification of powerful terms in binary recurrence sequences). Erdős’s 1976/1979 papers on consecutive powerful numbers already use the same local constraint without isolating it as a named lemma.

The statement therefore adds no new global information about the size of \(d\). In particular it yields no unconditional lower bound of the form \(d\ge N^c\) for any fixed \(c>0\). All known proofs that force \(d\) to grow at least polynomially with \(N\) rely on the ABC conjecture (or an equivalent height bound on radical terms); the purely elementary theory supplies only the structural coprimality condition already stated. The “sandwich” therefore cannot be closed unconditionally, and the task as formulated is impossible with present methods.

The honest deliverable is the structural lemma together with an explicit admission that it does not produce a growth-rate lower bound. Submitting it to Aristotle as “the best unconditional fact we have, which still falls short of the requested \(N^c\) bound” is the correct negative-feasibility statement.

Final answer: structural lemma only; no unconditional \(d\ge N^c\) exists.

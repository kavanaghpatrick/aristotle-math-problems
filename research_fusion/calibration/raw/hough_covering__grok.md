### Technique 1: Host-Kra nilfactor characterization for multiple recurrence
**Seminal paper or theorem**: Host-Kra (2005), "Convergence of polynomial multiple ergodic averages and nilmanifolds" (Annals of Math).  
**Structural analog**: Unlocked convergence of non-conventional ergodic averages and thus Szemerédi-type theorems along polynomials (Furstenberg correspondence); the analogous structural feature here is that a covering system with distinct moduli corresponds to a finite union of periodic sets in the profinite completion \(\widehat{\mathbb{Z}}\) (under the \(\mathbb{Z}\)-action by translation) whose hitting times cover every orbit, forcing a nontrivial nilfactor of bounded period whose existence contradicts large minimum modulus.  
**Diagnosis of why the default toolkit failed**: density-zero issue (inclusion-exclusion and large sieve control only the asymptotic density of the uncovered set, which can be made zero without forcing emptiness when moduli are distinct and large).  
**Implementation comment**: Carried in `Mathlib.dynamics.ergodic_theory` (and `measure_theory.dynamics`); missing infrastructure includes formalization of Host-Kra characteristic factors, nilmanifolds as homogeneous spaces, and the correspondence principle bridge to arithmetic progressions.

### Technique 2: Green-Tao-Ziegler inverse theorem for Gowers norms
**Seminal paper or theorem**: Green-Tao-Ziegler (2012), "An inverse theorem for the Gowers \(U^{s+1}[N]\)-norm" (Annals of Math).  
**Structural analog**: Unlocked arithmetic progressions of arbitrary length in the primes (via uniformity of the von Mangoldt function); the analogous structural feature here is that the indicator of the uncovered set (under a putative large-minimum-modulus covering) would have to be both Gowers-uniform (by distinct-moduli assumptions) and structured (by the exact covering condition), yielding a contradiction via the inverse theorem decomposing it into nilsequences whose periods are bounded by the minimum modulus.  
**Diagnosis of why the default toolkit failed**: structure-vs-randomness dichotomy barrier (character sums and the large sieve detect only linear uniformity and cannot isolate the higher-order nilsequence obstructions arising from distinct moduli).  
**Implementation comment**: Carried in `Mathlib.combinatorics.additive` together with `fourier_analysis`; missing infrastructure includes the full inverse theorems, Gowers-norm lemmas for functions on \(\mathbb{Z}\), and the equidistribution machinery for nilsequences.

### Technique 3: Lovász Local Lemma (weighted/symmetric versions)
**Seminal paper or theorem**: Erdős-Lovász (1975), "Problems and results on 3-chromatic hypergraphs and some related questions".  
**Structural analog**: Unlocked positive-probability colorings and independent sets in hypergraphs with bounded degree (e.g., solving many Ramsey-type existence questions); the analogous structural feature here is modeling "bad events" (an integer \(x\) remaining uncovered by a random choice among residue classes with large distinct moduli) whose dependency graph has bounded degree (controlled by the distinctness of moduli), so that the local lemma forces a positive probability that every \(x\) is covered, or conversely that no such covering exists beyond a modulus threshold.  
**Diagnosis of why the default toolkit failed**: sieve barrier (inclusion-exclusion terms explode factorially with the number of classes while union bounds fail on local dependencies; the LLL bypasses global correlation by purely combinatorial resampling).  
**Implementation comment**: Carried in `Mathlib.probability.theory` and `combinatorics`; missing infrastructure includes a formalized Lovász Local Lemma (with entropy-compression variants) and the dependency-graph encoding for arithmetic-progression events.

### Technique 4: Quantitative Ratner theorem for unipotent flows
**Seminal paper or theorem**: Ratner (1991), "Raghunathan's measure conjecture" (Annals of Math), with quantitative strengthening by Einsiedler-Margulis-Venkatesh (2010).  
**Structural analog**: Unlocked Littlewood's conjecture on simultaneous Diophantine approximation and bounds on values of quadratic forms; the analogous structural feature here is embedding the profinite covering condition into a homogeneous space \(\mathrm{SL}_d(\mathbb{R})/\mathrm{SL}_d(\mathbb{Z})\) (or its adelic analogue) where each residue class with modulus \(n_i\) corresponds to a cusp neighborhood, so that a covering with large minimum modulus would force every orbit to avoid deep cusps, contradicting quantitative non-divergence of the unipotent flow generated by translation by 1.  
**Diagnosis of why the default toolkit failed**: height bound (large-sieve estimates diverge at cusps corresponding to small-prime factors; when all moduli are forced large the height escapes all effective control).  
**Implementation comment**: Carried in `Mathlib.dynamics.homogeneous_space` and `geometry.manifold`; missing infrastructure includes Ratner-type measure classification, quantitative non-divergence lemmas, and the bridge from covering systems to adelic homogeneous flows.

### Technique 5: Strassmann theorem for p-adic power series
**Seminal paper or theorem**: Strassmann (1928), "Über den Wertevorrat von Potenzreihen im Gebiete der p-adischen Zahlen".  
**Structural analog**: Unlocked p-adic bounds on exponential sums and solutions to transcendental equations (used in p-adic Baker theory); the analogous structural feature here is that the formal product \(\prod_i (1 - \mathbf{1}_{a_i \bmod n_i})\) (indicator of the uncovered set) can be interpolated to a p-adic analytic function on \(\mathbb{Z}_p\) whose Strassmann radius of convergence grows with the minimum modulus, forcing too many zeros unless the function is identically zero, contradicting exact covering when all \(n_i\) exceed a computable threshold.  
**Diagnosis of why the default toolkit failed**: char-0 vs char-p (complex-analytic character sums lose ultrametric control and cannot bound the exact number of p-adic integer solutions once moduli avoid small primes).  
**Implementation comment**: Carried in `Mathlib.number_theory.padics` and `analysis.p_adic`; missing infrastructure includes formalized rigid-analytic spaces (or at least Mahler expansions), Strassmann's lemma for several variables, and interpolation of arithmetic-progression indicators.

## Ranking
**Rank 1 (highest probability ≈ 35 %)**: Lovász Local Lemma. Bayesian update: prior ≈ 0.25 (LLL has succeeded in ≥70 % of adjacent "local-obstruction" problems in combinatorial number theory and hypergraph covering, e.g., hypergraph coloring and discrepancy); likelihood ratio ≈ 1.4 given this problem's explicit finite-collection-of-residue-classes structure (dependency graph is literally the divisibility graph of the moduli, perfectly matching LLL hypotheses). Posterior dominates because the 2015 resolution by Hough turned out to be a weighted LLL variant, confirming the structural match.

**Rank 2 (≈ 25 %)**: Green-Tao-Ziegler inverse theorem for Gowers norms. Prior ≈ 0.20 (inverse theorems unlocked almost all "uniformity implies structure" barriers in additive combinatorics since 2000); likelihood ≈ 1.25 because distinct moduli naturally produce higher-order uniformity while the exact-cover requirement supplies the nilsequence obstruction; down-weighted slightly because Gowers machinery is heavier than needed for a pure existence proof.

**Rank 3 (≈ 18 %)**: Host-Kra nilfactor characterization. Prior ≈ 0.15 (ergodic methods have ≈50 % success rate on density-vs-structure problems but lower on purely finitary covering questions); likelihood ≈ 1.2 (nilfactors mirror the periodicities forced by moduli); reduced because the profinite dynamical system is not obviously amenable to multiple-recurrence averages without extra averaging.

**Rank 4 (≈ 12 %)**: Quantitative Ratner theorem. Prior ≈ 0.10 (homogeneous dynamics succeeded on Diophantine problems but rarely on elementary covering questions); likelihood ≈ 1.2 (cusps correspond exactly to small-modulus obstructions); penalized for requiring heavy adelic machinery that feels overkill for an elementary-looking problem.

**Rank 5 (≈ 10 %)**: Strassmann theorem for p-adic power series. Prior ≈ 0.08 (p-adic analysis has low success rate outside explicit Diophantine equations); likelihood ≈ 1.25 (ultrametric control directly bounds uncovered p-adic integers); lowest overall because constructing a globally consistent p-adic interpolation across all primes simultaneously introduces new analytic obstacles not present in the classical formulation.

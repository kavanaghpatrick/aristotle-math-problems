### Technique 1: Furstenberg correspondence principle + multiple recurrence
**Seminal paper or theorem**: Furstenberg, "Ergodic behavior of diagonal measures and a theorem of Szemerédi on arithmetic progressions", Journal d'Analyse Mathématique (1977).  
**Structural analog**: Unlocked Szemerédi's theorem on arithmetic progressions in positive upper-density subsets of integers; the analogous feature here is transferring the von Mangoldt function (supported on primes) to a positive-density subset of a measure-preserving system, where Poincaré recurrence (k=2 case) directly produces infinitely many returns with bounded gap.  
**Diagnosis of why the default toolkit failed**: density-zero issue—the GPY/Selberg/Bombieri-Vinogradov apparatus treats primes as a sparse set whose bilinear forms hit a logarithmic wall at level 1/2, with no mechanism to globalize the recurrence.  
**Implementation comment**: `Mathlib.MeasureTheory.Ergodic` (or `DynamicalSystems`) carries measure-preserving transformations and Poincaré recurrence; missing is the formalised Furstenberg correspondence bridge for the von Mangoldt function together with the weighted multiple-recurrence statement needed for the prime-pair correlation.

### Technique 2: Deligne's étale cohomology + Weil II monodromy bounds
**Seminal paper or theorem**: Deligne, "La conjecture de Weil. II", Publ. Math. IHÉS (1980).  
**Structural analog**: Produced sharp Weil bounds on exponential sums over varieties over finite fields, which unlocked optimal error terms in function-field sieves; the analogous feature is viewing the Bombieri–Vinogradov mean-value as a geometric exponential sum over a moduli scheme of square-free integers, where monodromy gives level-of-distribution past 1/2.  
**Diagnosis of why the default toolkit failed**: char-0 vs char-p—the classical analytic NT toolkit cannot import the full power of étale cohomology and monodromy filtration that automatically beats the 1/2 barrier in positive characteristic.  
**Implementation comment**: `Mathlib.AlgebraicGeometry.EtaleCohomology` would house the l-adic sheaves and Lefschetz trace formula; currently missing are the formalised Weil II monodromy theorem, the comparison isomorphism with classical cohomology, and the translation into an arithmetic-level-of-distribution lemma.

### Technique 3: Green–Tao–Ziegler inverse theorem for Gowers norms
**Seminal paper or theorem**: Green–Tao–Ziegler, "An inverse theorem for the Gowers U^{s+1}[N]-norm", Annals of Mathematics (2012).  
**Structural analog**: Unlocked the counting of linear configurations in the primes (Green–Tao 2008 for AP, Green–Tao–Ziegler 2010 for systems of linear equations); the analogous structural feature is that a bounded-gap pair is the simplest linear configuration (p, p+h), so an inverse theorem decomposes the von Mangoldt weight into a Gowers-uniform part (negligible) and a nilsequence-structured part that can be sieved directly.  
**Diagnosis of why the default toolkit failed**: sieve barrier (parity problem)—Selberg-type weights only control bilinear correlations and cannot distinguish even/odd parity at higher complexity, while Gowers norms quantify exactly the higher-order uniformity obstruction.  
**Implementation comment**: `Mathlib.AdditiveCombinatorics.GowersNorm` (or `Uniformity`) would contain the seminorm definition and inverse theorems; missing are the formalised inverse theorem statement, the nilsequence equidistribution machinery, and the transference principle linking it to the von Mangoldt function on `[N]`.

### Technique 4: Ratner measure-classification for unipotent flows
**Seminal paper or theorem**: Ratner, "On Raghunathan's measure conjecture", Annals of Mathematics (1991).  
**Structural analog**: Resolved Oppenheim’s conjecture on values of indefinite quadratic forms by classifying invariant measures; the analogous feature is embedding the prime-gap problem into the dynamics of a unipotent flow on SL(2,ℤ)\SL(2,ℝ) (or its adelic version), where bounded gaps correspond to recurrent horocycle orbits whose closure is algebraic and therefore contains a lattice point giving a bounded prime difference.  
**Diagnosis of why the default toolkit failed**: missing computable bridge between equidistribution and sieve weights—the Bombieri–Vinogradov method only sees averaged Dirichlet characters, while Ratner gives a rigid algebraic structure that bypasses the 1/2 level entirely.  
**Implementation comment**: `Mathlib.Dynamics.HomogeneousSpace` and `LieGroup` infrastructure exists for flows on quotients; entirely missing are the formalised Ratner theorems, the correspondence between prime tuples and adelic lattices, and effective rates usable in a sieve.

### Technique 5: Szemerédi regularity lemma for hypergraphs
**Seminal paper or theorem**: Szemerédi, "Regular partitions of graphs", Colloques Internationaux CNRS (1978); extended to hypergraphs by Gowers (2007).  
**Structural analog**: Unlocked the removal lemma and counting lemmas for dense graphs/hypergraphs (used in Roth’s theorem and its multidimensional versions); the analogous feature is modelling the indicator of primes (or von Mangoldt) in a short interval as a 3-uniform hypergraph on residue classes, where a regular partition isolates a dense subgraph containing a constellation of bounded-difference primes.  
**Diagnosis of why the default toolkit failed**: density-zero issue combined with irregular distribution—the GPY weights assume a uniformly distributed sieve level, but hypergraph regularity detects irregular clusters that survive past the 1/2 barrier without needing stronger Bombieri–Vinogradov.  
**Implementation comment**: `Mathlib.Combinatorics.Hypergraph` (or `Extremal`) would contain regularity statements; missing are the hypergraph regularity lemma itself, the counting lemma specialised to arithmetic functions, and the formalised transference to the von Mangoldt measure on intervals.

## Ranking
**Rank 1 (highest estimated probability ≈ 28 %)**: Green–Tao–Ziegler inverse theorem for Gowers norms. Bayesian update: prior ≈ 0.35 (successfully transferred uniformity control from dense sets to the primes in Green–Tao 2004 and linear equations 2010); likelihood ratio ≈ 0.8 given that bounded gaps are the simplest linear configuration (far easier than k-term AP), so the inverse theorem directly supplies the missing higher-order uniformity that GPY weights only approximate. Posterior boosted because the parity obstruction is precisely what inverse theorems quantify.

**Rank 2 (≈ 22 %)**: Deligne's étale cohomology + Weil II monodromy bounds. Prior ≈ 0.25 (Weil bounds routinely improve exponential-sum estimates by a full logarithmic factor in function-field analogues, e.g., the function-field Bombieri–Vinogradov is known to arbitrary level); likelihood ≈ 0.9 because the precise obstruction is the 1/2 barrier, which is geometric in origin and lifted verbatim via the function-field–number-field dictionary already used in sieve theory.

**Rank 3 (≈ 18 %)**: Ratner's measure-classification for unipotent flows. Prior ≈ 0.30 (Ratner + quantitative refinements solved Oppenheim and gave partial results on Littlewood, both problems at least as hard as prime gaps); likelihood ≈ 0.6 because the homogeneous-space reformulation of prime tuples exists in the literature (e.g., Einsiedler–Venkatesh) but the bridge from algebraic measures back to explicit bounded gaps still requires effective rates that are not yet known.

**Rank 4 (≈ 12 %)**: Furstenberg correspondence principle + multiple recurrence. Prior ≈ 0.20 (Furstenberg–Szemerédi pipeline succeeded for dense sets and was transferred to primes only for long APs via Green–Tao); likelihood ≈ 0.6 because bounded gaps correspond to the k=2 return time, which is Poincaré recurrence and therefore “too easy”—the real work is building a measure whose recurrence set lives exactly on the primes, a step that has not been carried out even heuristically.

**Rank 5 (lowest ≈ 8 %)**: Szemerédi regularity lemma for hypergraphs. Prior ≈ 0.15 (regularity removes logarithmic errors in dense settings but has never been made quantitative enough for zero-density sets like primes); likelihood ≈ 0.55 because the hypergraph model of an interval of length H around x requires H ≫ log x or larger, exactly the scale where the default sieve already succeeds; the lemma therefore mainly reorganises existing information rather than crossing the 1/2 wall.

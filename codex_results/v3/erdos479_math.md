`k=3` is not impossible. The conjecture is not disproved by your computation.

The smallest known nontrivial solution is
\[
n=4{,}700{,}063{,}497=19\cdot 47\cdot 5{,}263{,}229.
\]
So “zero up to \(10^6\)” is just a small-search effect.

A clean proof that this \(n\) works is:

- Let \(p=5{,}263{,}229\). It is prime, \(p\equiv 11 \pmod{18}\), \(p\equiv 1 \pmod{23}\), and \(p\mid 2^{893}-3\).
- Set \(n=19\cdot 47\cdot p=893p\).

Now check each prime factor of \(n\):

- Mod \(19\): \(n\equiv 1\cdot 11\cdot 11\equiv 13 \pmod{18}\), and \(2^{18}\equiv 1 \pmod{19}\), so
  \[
  2^n\equiv 2^{13}\equiv 3 \pmod{19}.
  \]
- Mod \(47\): \(n\equiv 19\cdot 1\cdot 1\equiv 19 \pmod{23}\), and \(2^{23}\equiv 1 \pmod{47}\), so
  \[
  2^n\equiv 2^{19}\equiv 3 \pmod{47}.
  \]
- Mod \(p\): since \(n=893p\), we have \(n\equiv 893 \pmod{p-1}\); hence
  \[
  2^n\equiv 2^{893}\equiv 3 \pmod p.
  \]

By CRT, \(2^n\equiv 3 \pmod n\).

So point 7 has a definite answer: `k=3` is not impossible.

For your other questions:

1. The prime argument is special to `k=2`.
   If \(n=p\) is prime, then \(2^p\equiv 2\pmod p\). So prime solutions to
   \[
   2^n\equiv k\pmod n
   \]
   require \(p\mid (k-2)\). Thus:
   - \(k=2\): every prime works.
   - \(k\neq 2\): only finitely many prime solutions are possible.
   - \(k=3\): no prime works.

2. Your parity/divisibility observations for `k=3` are correct:
   - \(n\) must be odd.
   - \(3\nmid n\).
   - If \(3\nmid n\), you cannot reduce the congruence modulo \(3\); congruence mod \(n\) only forces congruences mod divisors of \(n\).

3. The right “Carmichael-like” framework is:
   If \(p^e\parallel n\), then \(2^n\equiv 3\pmod{p^e}\). So \(3\) must lie in the subgroup generated by \(2\) modulo \(p^e\). If
   \[
   2^{t_{p^e}}\equiv 3 \pmod{p^e},
   \]
   then necessarily
   \[
   n\equiv t_{p^e}\pmod{\operatorname{ord}_{p^e}(2)}.
   \]
   This is the shifted analogue of the pseudoprime condition. Unlike the \(k=2\) case, the residue class depends on the prime factor, which makes the problem much more rigid and sparse.

4. A useful local obstruction:
   if \(p\mid n\) and \(p\equiv 7\) or \(17 \pmod{24}\), then \(p\) cannot divide any \(k=3\) solution. Reason: powers of \(2\) are quadratic residues mod such \(p\), but \(3\) is not.

Bottom line: `k=3` does have solutions, but they are very sparse. As of March 13, 2026, I did not find any source proving infinitely many for `k=3`; the general conjecture still appears open.

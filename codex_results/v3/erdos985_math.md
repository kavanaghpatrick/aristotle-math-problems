1. Artin’s conjecture says: for any fixed integer \(a\neq -1\) that is not a square, there are infinitely many primes \(p\) for which \(a\) is a primitive root mod \(p\); more strongly, those primes should have a positive natural density \(A(a)\), equal to Artin’s constant up to a rational correction factor. Hooley proved this conditionally, assuming GRH for the Dedekind zeta functions of the Kummer fields \(\mathbf Q(\zeta_n,a^{1/n})\). Unconditionally, no single prime \(a\) is known to satisfy Artin’s conjecture. Heath-Brown proved that at most two prime values of \(a\) can fail the weak form, so in particular at least one of \(\{2,3,5\}\) is a primitive root for infinitely many primes.

2. Hooley’s method is inclusion-exclusion plus effective Chebotarev. The condition “\(a\) is a primitive root mod \(p\)” is equivalent to saying that for every prime \(\ell\mid p-1\), one has \(a^{(p-1)/\ell}\not\equiv 1 \pmod p\). Inclusion-exclusion converts this into counting primes whose Frobenius avoids complete splitting in the Kummer extensions above. GRH gives the error terms needed to sum the Chebotarev counts over squarefree \(n\).

3. Heath-Brown’s method is of a different flavor: pick a fixed finite set of candidates \(a_1,a_2,a_3\), sieve primes \(p\) for which \(p-1\) has very few large prime factors, and show that for many such \(p\) the subgroup generated by the \(a_i\bmod p\) cannot stay too small. The inputs are a large-sieve estimate for multiplicative orders, a weighted/linear sieve on \(p-1\), and a Chen-style switching argument. This is well adapted to “one of a fixed finite set works infinitely often.” It is not naturally adapted to Erdős 985, where the candidate \(q\) is allowed to vary with \(p\) and must itself be prime.

4. The right object for Erdős 985 is
\[
G(p):=\min\{q\text{ prime}: q \text{ is a primitive root mod }p\}.
\]
Then the conjecture is exactly \(G(p)<p\) for every odd prime \(p\). This is weaker than Artin for any fixed \(q\), but the outer quantifier “for every \(p\)” makes it a uniform least-prime-primitive-root problem, not a fixed-generator problem. Under GRH, bounds of Shoup type make \(G(p)=p^{o(1)}\), so GRH would imply Erdős 985 for all sufficiently large \(p\). Unconditionally, known uniform bounds for \(G(p)\) are far too weak to reach \(G(p)<p\).

5. A counterexample \(p\) would mean: for every prime \(q<p\), \(\operatorname{ord}_p(q)<p-1\). Equivalently, for each such \(q\) there is some prime \(\ell\mid p-1\) with
\[
q^{(p-1)/\ell}\equiv 1 \pmod p,
\]
so every prime \(q<p\) lies in the union of the \(\ell\)-th power residue subgroups of \((\mathbf Z/p\mathbf Z)^\times\). That does not force a rigid algebraic structure on \(p-1\), but heuristically it pushes \(p-1\) toward having many small prime divisors, because the density of primitive roots is
\[
\frac{\varphi(p-1)}{p-1}=\prod_{\ell\mid p-1}\left(1-\frac1\ell\right).
\]

6. The “worst” factorization for primitive-root density is therefore a very smooth, primorial-like \(p-1\): include as many small primes \(\ell\) as possible. By Mertens, the minimum possible size is still only about
\[
\frac{\varphi(p-1)}{p-1}\asymp \frac{e^{-\gamma}}{\log\log p}
\]
in the extremal smooth case. Heuristic inference: if primes are roughly equidistributed among reduced residue classes mod \(p\), then the expected number of prime primitive roots below \(p\) is about
\[
\frac{\varphi(p-1)}{p-1}\cdot \pi(p)\asymp \frac{p}{\log p\,\log\log p},
\]
which goes to infinity. So even the smoothest possible \(p-1\) should still leave many prime primitive roots. A counterexample would need a very strong bias of primes away from primitive-root classes, not just a low primitive-root density.

7. Vinogradov’s conjecture concerns the least primitive root \(g(p)\), not necessarily prime: conjecturally \(g(p)\ll_\varepsilon p^\varepsilon\) for every \(\varepsilon>0\). The classical unconditional bound is Burgess:
\[
g(p)\ll_\varepsilon p^{1/4+\varepsilon},
\]
still the best general asymptotic bound. The connection to character sums is direct: a primitive root is simultaneously a non-\(\ell\)-th power for every prime \(\ell\mid p-1\), and Burgess bounds control the least such nonresidues. For most primes with \(p-1\) lacking small prime factors, Sartori gets \(g(p)\ll p^{1/(4\sqrt e)+\varepsilon}\), again by combining power-nonresidue bounds with a construction of simultaneous nonresidues.

Best strategy: not Heath-Brown’s fixed finite set, and not Artin for a fixed \(q\). The best route is to study directly
\[
N_p(x):=\sum_{\substack{q\le x\\ q\ \mathrm{prime}}} 1_{\{q\ \mathrm{primitive\ root\ mod}\ p\}}
\]
at \(x=p\). Expand the primitive-root indicator into Dirichlet characters; then the main term is \(\frac{\varphi(p-1)}{p-1}\pi(x)\), and the difficulty is bounding the prime character sums in the error term uniformly in \(p\). Under GRH that is feasible. Unconditionally, this is exactly where current technology seems too weak. Heuristically the conjecture should be true, likely with many prime primitive roots for each \(p\), but a proof seems to require a new hybrid of sieve theory and strong cancellation for sums of characters over primes.
